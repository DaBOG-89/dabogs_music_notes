The Tuba

A tuba is a large brass wind instrument that produces the lowest and deepest notes in the brass family. It gives music a strong, full foundation and is often called the “bass of the brass section.”

The tuba is made of brass and has a wide conical tube that curves around into a large flared bell. The player blows air through a mouthpiece and presses valves to change notes. Because of its size, the tuba produces a deep, rich, and resonant sound.

Made of brass with three to six valves. Produces the lowest tones in the brass family. Has a large bell and wide tubing. Played sitting down, with the bell facing upward.

The tuba is used in orchestras, concert bands, brass bands, and military bands. It often plays the bass line, supporting the rhythm and harmony, but it can also perform solos in some pieces.

A tuba player is called a tubist or tuba player.
